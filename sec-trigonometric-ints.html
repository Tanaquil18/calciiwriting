    Section 1.1 Trigonometric Integrals

    Objectives

    • How can the use of trigonometric identities expand the kinds of functions we can integrate?
    • How do we recognize which trigonometric identities to use in different integration problems?
    • How can we integrate functions which don’t appear to involve trigonometry by making use of a trigonometric substitution?
    We have learned how to use the integration techniques of substitution and integration-by-parts to build upon basic antiderivatives and greatly increase the kinds of functions we can integrate. For example, we know that
    \begin{equation*} \int \cos(x)\, dx = \sin(x)+C\text{,} \end{equation*}
    and we also can calculate that
    \begin{equation*} \int \sin^3(x)\cos(x)\, dx = \frac{\sin^4(x)}{4}+C\text{,} \end{equation*}
    using substitution, since we can let \(u=\sin(x)\) so that \(du=\cos(x)\, dx\text{,}\) which transforms the integral into
    \begin{equation*} \int u^3\, du = \frac{u^4}{4}+C\text{.} \end{equation*}
    But what if we wanted to integrate
    \begin{equation*} \int \sin^3(x)\cos^2(x)\, dx \end{equation*}
    or
    \begin{equation*} \int \sin^2(x)\cos^2(x)\, dx \end{equation*}
    or
    \begin{equation*} \int \sec^3(x)\tan^2(x)\, dx\text{?} \end{equation*}

    Subsection 1.1.1 Integrals Involving Sine and Cosine

    Example 1.1.1. Substitution, with an Additional Step.

    Evaluate
    \begin{equation*} \int \sin^3(x)\cos^2(x)\, dx. \end{equation*}
    Answer: We try finding a function-derivative pair, but things don’t look good, since if \(u=\sin(x)\) then \(du=\cos(x)\, dx\text{,}\) not \(\cos^2(x)\text{.}\) Similarly, if \(u=\cos(x)\) then \(du=-\sin(x)\, dx\text{,}\) not \(-\sin^3(x)\text{.}\) Trying combinations of \(\sin(x)\) and \(\cos(x)\) gets messy quickly, since we’d need to use product rule to calculate the derivative, and the integrand is not a sum of terms.
    However, we do know something about how \(\sin(x)\) and \(\cos(x)\) are related to each other. Specifically, the Fundamental Trigonometric Identity says that
    \begin{equation} \sin^2(x)+\cos^2(x)=1.\tag{1.1.1} \end{equation}
    What if we tried a substitution, and then if we still had either \(\sin^2(x)\) or \(\cos^2(x)\) in our integral, we used the Fundamental Trigonometric Identity to rewrite it?
    Let’s try \(u=\cos(x)\text{,}\) so that \(du=-\sin(x)\, dx\text{.}\) Then
    \begin{align*} \int \sin^3(x)\cos^2(x)\, dx \amp = \int \sin^2(x)\sin(x)\cos^2(x)\, dx \\ \amp = \int \sin^2(x)\cos^2(x)\big(\sin(x)\, dx\big) \\ \amp = \int \big(1-\cos^2(x)\big)\cdot\cos^2(x)\cdot\big(\sin(x)\, dx\big) \\ \amp = \int \big(1-u^2\big)\cdot u^2\cdot(-du) \\ \amp = \int (u^4-u^2)\, du \\ \amp = \frac{u^5}{5}-\frac{u^3}{3}+C \\ \amp = \frac{\cos^5(x)}{5}-\frac{\cos^3(x)}{3}+C \text{.} \end{align*}
    It worked! And it was mostly using our familiar technique of substitution, once we recognized when we needed to use a trigonometric identity to rewrite something we had remaining in our integral.
    This same strategy can be used whenever powers of sine and cosine (with the same argument) are multiplied together and one of the exponents is an odd, positive integer: one power can be factored out to be the \(du\text{,}\) an odd minus 1 is now even, and so the remaining powers can be turned into an expression of \(u\) using the Fundamental Trigonometric Identity.

    Sine and Cosine, at least one of them odd powered.

    Suppose you have an integral of the form
    \begin{equation*} \int \sin^m(z)\cos^n(z)\, dz \end{equation*}
    where either \(m\) or \(n\) (or both) is an odd, positive integer. Without loss of generality, let’s say \(n\text{,}\) the exponent of cosine, is odd and positive.
    • Factor out one copy of the trig function with the odd power (in this case \(\cos(z)\)) to serve as the \(du\text{.}\)
    • Rewrite the remaining powers of \(\cos(z)\) (which originally had an odd exponent and will now have an even exponent) in terms of \(\sin(z)\) using the Fundamental Trigonometric Identity \(\cos^2(z)=1-\sin^2(z)\text{.}\)
    • Set \(u\) equal to \(\sin(z)\) (because \(\cos(z)\) originally had an odd exponent.)
    What if we have an integral involving a product of sines and cosines but neither of the functions has an odd, positive integer exponent? We have another method that can be used in the case that both sine and cosine have even, nonnegative integer exponents.

    Example 1.1.2. Sine and Cosine with Even Exponents.

    Evaluate
    \begin{equation*} \int \sin^2(x)\, dx. \end{equation*}
    Answer: If we rewrote \(\sin^2(x)\) in terms of \(\cos^2(x)\) using the Fundamental Trigonometric identity, then we wouldn’t have any copy of \(\sin(x)\) leftover to serve as the \(du\) if we made a \(u-\)substitution.
    However, there are two more trigonometric identities we can make use of, called the Half-angle identities
    \begin{align} \sin^2(x) \amp = \frac{1-\cos(2x)}{2} \tag{1.1.2}\\ \cos^2(x) \amp = \frac{1+\cos(2x)}{2} \tag{1.1.3} \end{align}
    Then
    \begin{align*} \int \sin^2(x)\, dx \amp = \int \frac{1-\cos(2x)}{2}\, dx \\ \amp = \frac{1}{2}\int \big(1-\cos(2x)\big)\, dx \\ \amp = \frac{1}{2}\big[x-\frac{\sin(2x)}{2}\big]+C \\ \amp = \frac{x}{2}-\frac{\sin(2x)}{4}+C \text{.} \end{align*}
    The Half-angle identity helped because it brought the exponent down by 1, and we know how to calculate antiderivatives for both \(1\) and \(\cos(2x)\text{.}\)

    Sine and Cosine, both of them even powered.

    Suppose you have an integral of the form
    \begin{equation*} \int \sin^m(z)\cos^n(z)\, dz \end{equation*}
    where both \(m\) and \(n\) are an even, nonnegative integers (one of the exponents could be \(0\)).
    • Use the Half-angle identities
      \begin{align*} \sin^2(z) \amp = \frac{1-\cos(2z)}{2} \\ \cos^2(z) \amp = \frac{1+\cos(2z)}{2} \end{align*}
      to reduce the degree of sine and cosine (this will also double whatever the argument originally was).
    • Multiply out the result and apply the Half-angle identities again, as many times as necessary.

    Subsection 1.1.2 More substitution with trig identities

    The main principle behind Sine and Cosine, at least one of them odd powered is that we are using substitution to integrate, and the Fundamental Trigonometric Identity to rewrite what remains in terms of the function in the function-derivative pair.
    We also encounter other pairings besides sine and cosine of a trigonometric function and its derivative (up to a constant). Let’s recall that if we divide both sides of the Fundamental Trigonometric Identity (1.1.1) by \(\cos^2(x)\text{,}\) we get
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\sin^2(x)}{\cos^2(x)}+\frac{\cos^2(x)}{\cos^2(x)}=\frac{1}{\cos^2(x)}, \end{equation*}
    and rewriting that to be in terms of \(\tan(x)=\frac{\sin(x)}{\cos(x)}\) and \(\sec(x)=\frac{1}{\cos(x)}\) means we get
    \begin{equation} \tan^2(x)+1=\sec^2(x)\tag{1.1.4} \end{equation}
    We also recall that the derivative of \(\tan(x)\) is \(\sec^2(x)\text{,}\) and that the derivative of \(\sec(x)\) is \(\sec(x)\tan(x)\text{.}\) If we have an integral with a product of powers of secant and tangent, as long as the exponents of one or the other satisfy certain conditions, we can do the same thing that we did with sine and cosine: make a \(u-\)substitution and use the (modified) Fundamental Trigonometric Identity to rewrite the other trig function in terms of the one we called \(u\text{.}\)

    Example 1.1.3. One Case of Secants and Tangents.

    Evaluate
    \begin{equation*} \int \frac{\tan^3(x)}{\sec^2(x)}\, dx. \end{equation*}
    Answer: We know that the derivative of \(\sec(x)\) is \(\sec(x)\tan(x)\text{,}\) and that even powers of tangent can be rewritten into secants using (1.1.4).
    Let \(u=\sec(x)\text{,}\) so that \(du=\sec(x)\tan(x)\, dx\text{.}\) We factor out a copy of \(\sec(x)\tan(x)\text{,}\) by splitting the tangents in the numerator and also by multiplying by \(\frac{\sec(x)}{\sec(x)}=1\text{.}\)
    \begin{align*} \int \frac{\tan^3(x)}{\sec^2(x)}\, dx \amp = \int \frac{\tan^2(x)\tan(x)}{\sec^2(x)}\cdot\frac{\sec(x)}{\sec(x)}\, dx \\ \amp = \int \frac{\tan^2(x)}{\sec^3(x)}\sec(x)\tan(x)\, dx \\ \amp = \int \frac{\sec^2(x)-1}{\sec^3(x)}\sec(x)\tan(x)\, dx \\ \amp = \int \frac{u^2-1}{u^3}\, du \\ \amp = \int \big( \frac{1}{u}-u^{-3}\big)\, du \\ \amp = \ln|u|+\frac{u^{-2}}{2}+C \\ \amp = \ln|\sec(x)|+\frac{\sec^{-2}(x)}{2}+C \\ \amp = \ln|\sec(x)|+\frac{\cos^2(x)}{2}+C\text{.} \end{align*}
    The key feature in the above example was that the exponent of tangent was odd and positive, so that we could factor out a copy of \(\sec(x)\tan(x)\text{.}\) If instead the exponent of secant was even and positive, we could have factored out a copy of \(\sec^2(x)\text{.}\)

    Secant and Tangent, even power of secant.

    Suppose you have an integral of the form
    \begin{equation*} \int \sec^m(z)\tan^n(z)\, dz \end{equation*}
    where \(m\) is an even, positive integer.
    • Factor out a copy of \(\sec^2(z)\) to serve as the \(du\) (because \(\sec^2(z)\) is the derivative of \(\tan(z)\)).
    • Rewrite the remaining still even powers of \(\sec(z)\) in terms of \(\tan(z)\) using (1.1.4).
    • Set \(u\) equal to \(\tan(z)\text{.}\)

    Secant and Tangent, odd power of tangent.

    Suppose you have an integral of the form
    \begin{equation*} \int \sec^m(z)\tan^n(z)\, dz \end{equation*}
    where \(n\) is an odd, positive integer.
    • Factor out a copy of \(\sec(z)\tan(z)\) to serve as the \(du\) (because \(\sec(z)\tan(z)\) is the derivative of \(\sec{z}\)).
    • Rewrite the remaining now even powers of \(\tan(z)\) in terms of \(\sec(z)\) using (1.1.4).
    • Set \(u\) equal to \(\sec(z)\text{.}\)
    Similarly, dividing both sides of the Fundamental Trigonometric Identity (1.1.1) by \(\sin^2(x)\) results in
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\sin^2(x)}{\sin^2(x)}+\frac{\cos^2(x)}{\sin^2(x)}=\frac{1}{\sin^2(x)}, \end{equation*}
    and rewriting that to be in terms of \(\cot(x)=\frac{\cos(x)}{\sin(x)}\) and \(\csc(x)=\frac{1}{\sin(x)}\) means we get
    \begin{equation} 1+\cot^2(x)=\csc^2(x)\tag{1.1.5} \end{equation}
    Note that since the derivative of \(\cot(z)\) is \(-\csc^2(z)\) and the derivative of \(\csc(z)\) is \(-\csc(z)\cot(z)\text{,}\) the relationships between \(\csc(z)\) and \(\cot(z)\) are exactly analogous to the relationships between \(\sec(z)\) and \(\tan(z)\text{.}\) The integrals we consider are of the same forms, and the methods for evaluating them are the same.

    Cosecant and Cotangent, even power of cosecant.

    Suppose you have an integral of the form
    \begin{equation*} \int \csc^m(z)\cot^n(z)\, dz \end{equation*}
    where \(m\) is an even, positive integer.
    • Factor out a copy of \(\csc^2(z)\) to serve as the \(du\) (because \(-\csc^2(z)\) is the derivative of \(\cot(z)\)).
    • Rewrite the remaining still even powers of \(\csc(z)\) in terms of \(\cot(z)\) using (1.1.5).
    • Set \(u\) equal to \(\cot(z)\text{.}\)

    Cosecant and Cotangent, odd power of cotangent.

    Suppose you have an integral of the form
    \begin{equation*} \int \csc^m(z)\cot^n(z)\, dz \end{equation*}
    where \(n\) is an odd, positive integer.
    • Factor out a copy of \(\csc(z)\cot(z)\) to serve as the \(du\) (because \(-\csc(z)\cot(z)\) is the derivative of \(\csc{z}\)).
    • Rewrite the remaining now even powers of \(\cot(z)\) in terms of \(\csc(z)\) using (1.1.5).
    • Set \(u\) equal to \(\csc(z)\text{.}\)

    Subsection 1.1.3 Trigonometric Substitution

    We’ve been using a lot of substitution to calculate trigonometric integals, and we’ve greatly expanded the kinds of integrals we can evaluate, as long as they involve trigonometric functions. There is a saying that might be especially applicable to mathematicians.
    If all you have is a hammer, everything looks like a nail.
    We try to solve problems using the tools we have. The quote might be implying that a hammer isn’t always the best choice, but the following activity is a rather beautiful example of mathematicians figuring out how to use the hammer of our ability to integrate trigonometric functions, even when the integral starts out not looking like it involves trigonometry at all.

    Activity 1.1.3.

    The method demonstrated in this activity is most useful when the integral cannot be calculated in another way, but we will consider an example which can also be done with \(u-\) substitution so that we can verify that we obtain the same answer.
    1. State the reason why \(u\)-substitution cannot be used on the integral
      \begin{equation*} \int \frac{1}{(x^2+4)^2} \, dx\text{.} \end{equation*}
    2. For the remainder of this activity, we consider
      \begin{equation*} \int \frac{x}{(x^2+4)^2} \, dx\text{.} \end{equation*}
      First, calculate the integral using \(u\)-substitution, with \(u = x^2+4\text{.}\)
    3. We recognize the term \(x^2+4\) is of the form \(x^2+a^2\text{,}\) which reminds us of the hypotenuse of a right triangle, whose legs are \(x\) and \(2\text{.}\) (We previously did something similar in Calculus I here
       1 
      https://activecalculus.org/single-alt/sec-2-6-inverse.html#sec-2-6-inverse-6-9-1
      ).
      Use the triangle to calculate \(\tan \theta\) and \(\sec \theta\) in terms of \(x\text{.}\)
    4. Use your expression for \(\tan \theta\) to solve for \(x\text{,}\) then differentiate to calculate \(dx\text{.}\)
    5. Use your expression for \(\sec \theta\) to solve for \(x^2+4\text{.}\)
    6. Substitute what you’ve found into
      \begin{equation*} \int \frac{x}{(x^2+4)^2} \, dx\text{,} \end{equation*}
      replacing \(x\text{,}\) \(dx\text{,}\) and \(x^2+4\) with expressions involving \(\theta\text{.}\)
    7. Calculate the trigonometric integral, using the method for when the exponent of tangent is odd.
    8. Finally, put the answer back in terms of \(x\text{,}\) using the triangle shown above, and/or the expressions you already calculated.
    There are three of these trigonometric substitutions we commonly use, depending on what the original integrand is.

    Subsection 1.1.4 Summary

    • We practice our integration techniques of \(u-\)substitution and integration by parts as applied to functions involving products of trigonometric functions.
    • We use the Fundamental Pythagorean Trigonometric Identity,
      \begin{equation*} \sin^2(z)+\cos^2(z)=1 \end{equation*}
      its two variations,
      \begin{align*} \tan^2(z)+1 \amp = \sec^2(z) \\ \cot^2(z)+1 \amp = \csc^2(z) \end{align*}
      and the two Half-angle Formulas
      \begin{align*} \sin^2(z) \amp = \frac{1-\cos(2z)}{2} \\ \cos^2(z) \amp = \frac{1+\cos(2z)}{2} \end{align*}
      to enable us to calculate antiderivatives of many functions that are a product of trigonometric functions.
    • We see how to use a trigonometric substitution to transform certain non-trigonometric integrals into trigonometric integrals.
