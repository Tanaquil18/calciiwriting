    Section 1.2 The Method of Partial Fractions

    Objectives

    • How can we integrate rational functions when substitution does not work?
      🔗
      🔗
    • How do we break a rational function up into the sum of simpler rational functions?
      🔗
      🔗
    • What condition must be met in order to rewrite a rational function in this way?
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    The need to integrate rational functions arises in differential equations and in other settings. If we are lucky, we can use substitution or integration by parts, but oftentimes neither of those methods can be used. In this section, we learn how to use algebra together with our past integration techniques to integrate rational functions.
    🔗

    Subsection 1.2.1 The Building Blocks of the Method

    We are interested in integrating functions which are polynomials divided by polynomials. There are some functions of this form we already know how to do using previous methods. Each of these integrals comes up frequently enough that we collect them here for reference.
    🔗

    Integrals commonly used in partial fractions problems.

    In all of the following formulas, let \(A, b\) and \(n\) be numbers, with \(n \neq 1\text{.}\)
    • Functions which have a linear term in the denominator and a constant in the numerator can be integrated without work since they’re almost a basic antiderivative.
      \begin{equation*} \int \frac{A}{x+b} \, dx = A\ln|x+b|+C \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    • Functions which have a power of a linear term in the denominator (\(n\neq 1 \)) and a constant in the numerator can be integrated using the power rule.
      \begin{equation*} \int \frac{A}{(x+b)^n} \, dx = \frac{A}{-n+1}(x+b)^{-n+1}+C \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    • Some integrals with a quadratic in the denominator can be done using substitution, since the numerator is a constant multiple of the derivative of the denominator.
      \begin{equation*} \int \frac{Ax}{x^2+b} \, dx = \frac{A}{2}\ln|x^2+b|+C \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    • Some integrals with a quadratic in the denominator and a constant in the numerator can be done using either trigonometric substitution or regular substitution, leading to the formula
      \begin{equation*} \int \frac{A}{x^2+b}dx = \frac{A}{\sqrt{b}}\arctan\left(\frac{x}{\sqrt{b}}\right)+C \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 1.2.1.

    The method of partial fractions we learn in this section could be summarized as the following: reduce all functions of the form “polynomial divided by polynomial” to sums of functions of the forms above, which we already know how to integrate.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 1.2.2 Starting the Method of Partial Fractions

    We already had the tools to calculate an integral like
    \begin{equation*} \int \frac{5x}{x^2+1} \, dx = \frac{5}{2}\ln|x^2+1|+C \end{equation*}
    because there is a function-derivative pair in the integrand. If we let \(u=x^2+1\) then \(du=2x\, dx\text{,}\) and \(5x\) is a constant multiple of \(2x\text{,}\) so we get
    \begin{align*} \int \frac{5x}{x^2+1} \, dx \amp = \frac{5}{2}\int \frac{1}{u}\, du \\ \amp = \frac{5}{2}\ln|u|+C \\ \amp = \frac{5}{2}\ln|x^2+1|+C \text{.} \end{align*}
    🔗
    However, what if there isn’t a function-derivative pair in the integrand? Consider
    \begin{equation*} \int \frac{5x}{x^2-x-2} \, dx\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Substitution does not help with integrating this function, since if we let \(u=x^2-x-2\) then \(du=(2x-1)\, dx\text{,}\) which is not a constant multiple of \(5x \, dx\text{.}\) We need to use a completely different strategy to solve this integral.
    🔗
    If we somehow knew that
    \begin{equation} \frac{5x}{x^2-x-2} = \frac{10/3}{x-2} + \frac{5/3}{x+1}\tag{1.2.1} \end{equation}
    then we could integrate both sides of the equation to get
    \begin{equation*} \int \frac{5x}{x^2-x-2} \, dx = \int \frac{10/3}{x-2} + \frac{5/3}{x+1} \, dx\text{,} \end{equation*}
    and the integral on the right involves two nearly-basic antiderivatives, giving us
    \begin{equation*} \int \frac{5x}{x^2-x-2} \, dx = \frac{10}{3} \ln|x-2| + \frac{5}{3}\ln|x+1| + C\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Setting aside the question of where the right-hand-side came from for a brief moment, we can verify that (1.2.1) is indeed true by recombining the right-hand-side of the equation using a common denominator.
    \begin{align*} \frac{\frac{10}{3}}{x-2} + \frac{\frac{5}{3}}{x+1} \amp = \frac{\frac{10}{3}\cdot(x+1)}{(x-2)\cdot(x+1)} + \frac{\frac{5}{3}\cdot(x-2)}{(x+1)\cdot(x-2)}\\ \amp =\frac{\frac{10}{3}x+\frac{10}{3}+\frac{5}{3}x-\frac{10}{3} }{(x-2)(x+1)}\\ \amp =\frac{ \frac{15}{3}x}{(x-2)(x+1)}\\ \amp =\frac{5x}{x^2-x-2} \end{align*}
    🔗
    However, we do want to answer the big question: how did we come up with
    \begin{equation*} \frac{5x}{x^2-x-2} = \frac{10/3}{x-2} + \frac{5/3}{x+1} \end{equation*}
    in the first place? And how can we do the same thing to other integrals (reduce a function we can’t integrate with the method of substitution to a sum of functions we can integrate more easily?)
    🔗
    One clue is that the denominator of the rational function in the integral factors into two linear terms,
    \begin{equation*} x^2-x-2 = (x-2)(x+1)\text{,} \end{equation*}
    and those are exactly the denominators of the simpler rational functions we used. We will proceed by assuming there is some expression that is equal to the rational function and then solve for what the exact expression is.
    🔗

    Example 1.2.2. Two linear terms.

    Show that
    \begin{equation*} \int \frac{5x}{x^2-x-2} \, dx = \frac{10}{3} \ln|x-2| + \frac{5}{3}\ln|x+1| + C\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Answer: We first factor the denominator \(x^2-x-2 = (x-2)(x+1)\) and then suppose that
    \begin{equation} \frac{5x}{(x-2)(x+1)} = \frac{A}{x-2} + \frac{B}{x+1}\tag{1.2.2} \end{equation}
    for some constants \(A\) and \(B\text{.}\) If this is true, the integral becomes much easier to calculate, since we can integrate both sides of (1.2.2) to get
    \begin{equation} \int \frac{5x}{x^2-x-2} \, dx = A \ln|x-2| + B\ln|x+1| + C\text{.}\tag{1.2.3} \end{equation}
    🔗
    We now start the process of solving for the values of \(A\) and \(B\text{.}\) Multiplying both sides of (1.2.2) by the integrand’s denominator \((x-2)(x+1)\text{,}\) we find that
    \begin{equation*} \frac{5x\cancel{(x-2)(x+1)}}{\cancel{(x-2)(x+1)}}= \frac{A(x+1)\cancel{(x-2)}}{\cancel{x-2}} + \frac{B\cancel{(x+1)}(x-2)}{\cancel{x+1}} \end{equation*}
    which means
    \begin{equation} 5x = A(x+1) + B(x-2)\text{.}\tag{1.2.4} \end{equation}
    🔗
    From here, we show one possible method of solving for the values of \(A\) and \(B\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Because (1.2.4) is true for every value of \(x\text{,}\) we can use inspired choices of specific \(x\)-values to help us find \(A\) and \(B\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Taking \(x = -1\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation*} 5(-1) = A(0) + B(-3)\text{,} \end{equation*}
    so \(B = \frac{5}{3}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Now setting \(x = 2\text{,}\) it follows that
    \begin{equation*} 5(2) = A(3) + B(0)\text{,} \end{equation*}
    so \(A = \frac{10}{3}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Thus, substituting the values of \(A\) and \(B\) into (1.2.3) we get
    \begin{equation*} \int \frac{5x}{x^2-x-2} \, dx = \frac{10}{3}\ln|x-2| + \frac{5}{3}\ln|x+1|+C\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 1.2.3 How the denominator factors matters

    The main questions to explore now are: when can we integrate a rational function by rewriting into terms like those above, and what does “terms like those” mean exactly?
    🔗

      1. Factor the denominator, and rewrite the integrand as a sum where each addend is a constant divided by a factor of the denominator. Use letters like \(A, B, D\) etc., or \(A_1, A_2, A_3\text{,}\) etc.
        🔗

        🔗

      🔗

      1. Calculate \(\int \frac{A}{x-1}dx+\int\frac{B}{x^2+1}dx\text{.}\)
        🔗

        🔗

        🔗
        🔗
      4. Why does this show that \(\frac{x^2+x+4}{(x^2+1)(x-1)} \neq \frac{A}{x-1}+\frac{B}{x^2+1}\) for any values of \(A\) and \(B\text{?}\) How might we change the partial fraction decomposition form to fix this?

    Remark 1.2.3.

    The form of the partial fraction decomposition depends on how the denominator factors.
    🔗
    🔗
    When we had a numerator of \(x^2+x+4\text{,}\) there are \(3\) numbers: the coefficient of the \(x^2\) term, the coefficient of the \(x\) term, and the constant term. Using \(\frac{A}{x-1}+\frac{B}{x^2+1}\) gave us \(2\) numbers to solve for, \(A\) and \(B\text{,}\) and in this case, it was impossible to find values of \(A\) and \(B\) to make the equality hold. However, we can modify the partial fraction form to introduce a third number.
    🔗

    Example 1.2.4. One quadratic and one linear term.

    Evaluate
    \begin{equation*} \int \frac{x^2+x+4}{(x^2+1)(x-1)} \, dx\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Answer: We suppose that
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x^2+x+4}{(x^2+1)(x-1)} = \frac{A}{x-1} + \frac{Bx+D}{x^2+1} \end{equation*}
    and note that this gives us \(3\) constants to work with.
     1 
    We avoid using \(C\) as a coefficient because we don’t want to get it confused with the \(+C\) at the end of an indefinite integral.
    Before we look for the values of the constants \(A\text{,}\) \(B\) and \(D\text{,}\) we will first integrate, using three of the building blocks in Integrals commonly used in partial fractions problems.
    \begin{align} \int \frac{x^2+x+4}{(x^2+1)(x-1)}\, dx \amp = \int \left( \frac{A}{x-1} + \frac{Bx+D}{x^2+1}\right)\, dx \tag{1.2.5}\\ \amp = \int \left(\frac{A}{x-1} + \frac{Bx}{x^2+1} + \frac{D}{x^2+1}\right)\, dx \tag{1.2.6}\\ \amp = A\ln|x-1| + \frac{B}{2}\ln|x^2+1| + D\arctan(x) +C \tag{1.2.7} \end{align}
    🔗
    We have obtained a general antiderivative, as long as we can find such numbers \(A\text{,}\) \(B\) and \(D\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Now that we’ve done the calculus part, we turn our attention to the algebra. Multiplying both sides of the integrand by \((x-1)(x^2+1)\text{,}\) we find that
    \begin{equation*} \frac{(x^2+x+4)\cancel{(x-1)(x^2+1)}}{\cancel{(x-1)(x^2+1)}}= \frac{A(x^2+1)\cancel{(x-1)}}{\cancel{x-1}} + \frac{(Bx+D)\cancel{(x^2+1)}(x-1)}{\cancel{x^2+1}} \end{equation*}
    which means
    \begin{equation} x^2+x+4 = A(x^2+1) + (Bx+D)(x-1)\text{.}\tag{1.2.8} \end{equation}
    🔗
    We have several ways of solving for values of \(A, B\) and \(D\) which make (1.2.8) true for all values of \(x\text{.}\) We could...
    • ... use the black box method. Use a solver like Wolfram Alpha or another computational tool.
      🔗
      🔗
    • ... set \(x\) equal to different numerical values, as we did in Example 1.2.2. We could set \(x=1\text{,}\) then \(x=0\text{,}\) and then \(x=-1\text{,}\) or choose any three values of \(x\) that we find easiest to work with.
      🔗
      🔗
    • ... compare coefficients of both sides of the equation and get a system of equations to solve.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    We choose to demonstrate the last method here. Multiplying out the right-hand side of (1.2.8) and grouping like terms together gives
    \begin{align*} x^2+x+4 \amp = A(x^2+1) + (Bx+D)(x-1) \\ \amp = Ax^2+A + Bx^2-Bx+Dx-D\\ \amp = (A+B)x^2+(-B+D)x+(A-D) \end{align*}
    Since the two sides must be equal as polynomials, equal for all values of \(x\text{,}\) this means that the corresponding coefficients must be equal.
    \begin{align*} x^2 \text{ terms} \amp : 1=A+B \amp \text{ because } x^2=(A+B)x^2 \\ x \text{ terms} \amp : 1=-B+D \amp \text{ because } x = (-B+D)x \\ \text{ constant terms} \amp : 4 = A-D \amp \end{align*}
    We now have a system of linear equations to solve, with three equations and three unknowns, which we can solve using any of the usual tools at our disposal. The fastest way of solving for \(A, B\) and \(D\) by hand is combining this linear system with the method of choosing strategic values of \(x\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Setting \(x=1\) in (1.2.8) yields
    \begin{align*} 1^2+1+4 \amp =A(1^2+1)+(B\cdot 1 +D)(1-1)\\ \amp = 2A+0\text{,} \end{align*}
    and thus \(6=2A\text{,}\) so
    \begin{equation*} A=3\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Now the system of equations with three unknowns is reduced to having only two unknowns.
    🔗
    Also, since \(A=3\text{,}\) and comparing coefficients of the \(x^2\) terms gave us that \(A+B=1\text{,}\) this means that \(3+B=1\text{,}\) so
    \begin{equation*} B=-2\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Similarly, since comparing the constant terms gave us that \(A-D=4\) and we know that \(A=3\text{,}\) this means that
    \begin{equation*} D=-1\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    We had already integrated in (1.2.7), and so substituting the values we’ve just found for \(A, B\) and \(D\) gives us our final answer of
    \begin{align*} \int \frac{x^2+x+4}{(x^2+1)(x-1)} \, dx \amp = 3\ln|x-1|+\frac{-2}{2}\ln|x^2+1|-1\arctan(x) +C\\ \amp = 3\ln|x-1|-\ln|x^2+1|-\arctan(x) +C. \end{align*}
    🔗
    🔗
    It’s worth noting that if we’ve had a little bit of matrix theory or linear algebra, we can use matrices to solve a system of linear equations, and that would give us
    \begin{align*} \amp A \amp \amp+ B \amp \amp \ \amp \amp=1 \\ \amp \amp \amp- B \amp \amp+ D \amp \amp=1 \\ \amp A \amp \amp \amp \amp-D \amp \amp=4 \end{align*}
    which becomes \(\begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 1 \amp 0 \amp 1 \\ 0 \amp -1 \amp 1 \amp 1 \\1 \amp 0 \amp -1 \amp 4 \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solving this using Gaussian elimination results in \(\begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 3 \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp 0 \amp -2 \\0 \amp 0 \amp 1 \amp -1 \end{bmatrix}\text{,}\) which does give us the same values of \(A=3\text{,}\) \(B=-2\text{,}\) and \(D=-1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    However, this matrix form tells us something more: that this system of equations will have a unique solution regardless of the starting coefficients of the quadratic in the numerator of the integrand.
    🔗
    Note that we don’t have to use matrices at all - we have other tools for solving a system of equations. However, this example generalizes and bears repeating: as long as the numerator of the integrand is less than or equal to degree \(2\text{,}\) and the denominator factors into a linear and an irreducible quadratic, we can find a partial fraction decomposition and calculate the integral by setting it up in this way, with a constant over the linear factor and a linear term over the quadratic factor.
     2 
    See later for what to do if the degree is greater than or equal to \(3\) with this same factored form of the denominator.
    🔗
    Furthermore, we can see the problem that occurred when we had the wrong form of the partial fraction decomposition. When we attempted to set \(\frac{x^2+x+4}{(x^2+1)(x-1)} = \frac{A}{x-1}+\frac{B}{x^2+1}\) and cleared the denominators, we got
    \begin{equation*} x^2+x+4 = A(x^2+1)+B(x-1) = Ax^2+Bx+A-B. \end{equation*}
    Setting corresponding coefficients equal gives us
    \begin{align*} \amp A \amp \amp \amp \amp=1 \\ \amp \amp \amp B \amp \amp=1 \\ \amp A \amp \amp-B \amp \amp=4 \end{align*}
    which becomes \(\begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 0 \amp 1 \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp 1 \\1 \amp -1 \amp 4 \end{bmatrix}\text{,}\) which row reduces to \(\begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 0 \amp 1 \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp 1 \\0 \amp 0 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix}\text{,}\) an inconsistent system (no possible solutions for \(A\) and \(B\text{.}\))
    🔗
    One of the important takeaways from this is that the form of the partial fraction decomposition matters.
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 1.2.4 General Method of Partial Fractions

    Let’s collect and summarize what we can say about the possible forms of partial fraction decompositions.
    🔗

    Partial Fraction Decomposition.

    Given any rational function with the degree of the numerator strictly less than the degree of the denominator, we can rewrite the function as a sum of simpler terms.
    🔗
    Factored Denominator Contains Partial Fraction Decomposition Contains
    \((ax+b)\) \(\frac{A}{ax+b}\)
    \((ax+b)^n\) \(\frac{A_1}{ax+b}+\frac{A_2}{(ax+b)^2}+\cdots + \frac{A_n}{(ax+b)^n}\)
    \((ax^2+bx+c)\) \(\frac{Ax+B}{ax^2+bx+c}\)
    \((ax^2+bx+c)^n\) \(\frac{A_1x+B_1}{ax^2+bx+c}+\frac{A_2x+B_2}{(ax^2+bx+c)^2}+\cdots + \frac{A_nx+B_n}{(ax^2+bx+c)^n}\)
    In words, the symbols above say how to rewrite a rational function as a sum based on how the denominator factors:
    linear factor
    🔗
    constant divided by the linear term
    🔗
    linear factor raised to an exponent
    🔗
    sum of terms consisting of a constant divided by every power of that linear term starting at \(1\) and going up to the value of the exponent
    🔗
    unfactorable quadratic
    🔗
    linear expression divided by the quadratic
    🔗
    unfactorable quadratic raised to an exponent
    🔗
    sum of terms consisting of a linear expression divided by every power of that quadratic starting at \(1\) and going up to the value of the exponent
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Checkpoint 1.2.5. Don’t Forget to Factor.

    What is the partial fraction decomposition of \(\frac{5x-2}{x^2-1}\text{?}\)
    🔗
    • \(\frac{A}{x^2-1}\)
      🔗
    • This would be true if the denominator was linear instead of quadratic.
    • \(\frac{Ax+B}{x^2-1}\)
      🔗
    • This would be true if the denominator was an unfactorable quadratic, but the denominator can be factored.
    • \(\frac{A}{x+1}+\frac{B}{x-1}\)
      🔗
    • Correct!
    🔗

    Checkpoint 1.2.6. Powers.

    What is the partial fraction decomposition of \(\frac{5x-2}{(x+1)(x+2)^2}\text{?}\)
    🔗
    • \(\frac{A}{x+1}+\frac{B}{x+2}\)
      🔗
    • This would be true if both exponents of the original denominator were 1, but there’s an exponent of 2.
    • \(\frac{A}{x+1}+\frac{Bx+D}{(x+2)^2}\)
      🔗
    • This would be true if the denominator had an unfactorable quadratic instead of a power of a linear term.
    • \(\frac{A}{x+1}+\frac{B}{(x+2)^2}\)
      🔗
    • This is not enough terms to account for all the possible original numerators when the denominator has a power of a linear term.
    • \(\frac{A}{x+1}+\frac{B}{x+2}+\frac{D}{(x+2)^2}\)
      🔗
    • Correct!
    🔗

    Checkpoint 1.2.7. Powers again.

    What is the partial fraction decomposition of \(\frac{5}{(x+2)^3}\text{?}\)
    🔗
    • \(\frac{A}{x+2}+\frac{B}{(x+2)^2}+\frac{D}{(x+2)^3}\)
      🔗
    • Technically correct, but not the best choice for this particular example. Look at the original numerator again.
    • \(\frac{A}{(x+2)^3}\)
      🔗
    • Correct! Because the original numerator was a constant, and thus in fact, A=5. (If this needed to be integrated, we can use the power rule straightaway, no partial fractions work is required.)
    🔗

    Subsection 1.2.5 When The Degree of the Numerator is Greater Than Or Equal to the Degree of the Denominator

    We noted above that these partial fraction decomposition forms have enough constants to solve for every possible numerator only when the degree of the numerator is strictly less than the degree of the denominator. What if the degree of the numerator is too large?
    🔗
    In that case, we use polynomial division to rewrite the original integrand as the sum of a polynomial and a fraction whose numerator does have smaller degree than the denominator. This is not unlike rewriting \(\frac{15}{4}\) (where the numerator is bigger than the denominator) as \(3+\frac{3}{4} \) (where not the numerator is smaller than the denominator.) The hard part is figuring out how many times the denominator goes into the numerator evenly.

    Subsection 1.2.6 Final Considerations

    An especially astute reader might notice that we said that the denominator can contain terms of the form \(ax^2+bx+c\) but we only considered terms of the form \(x^2+k\) in our examples and activities. The reason is that every \(ax^2+bx+c\) can be written as \(a\left( \left(x-\frac{b}{2a}\right)^2+k\right)\) if we recall how to complete the square from algebra class, and then using a substitution
     3 
    \(y=x-\frac{b}{2a}\) so that \(dy=dx\)
    turns this into some constant number multiplied by \(y^2+k\text{.}\)
    🔗
    But this turns a calculus problem into even more of an algebra problem, and the focus of this material is integration, not algebra.
    🔗
    You might also be wondering what happens if the denominator doesn’t factor into powers of linear and quadratic terms. It is a theorem beyond the scope of this class that every polynomial whose coefficients are real numbers factors into powers of linear and quadratic terms.
    🔗
    The result is that every rational function, regardless of the degree of the numerator or denominator, or how the denominator factors, can be integrated using some combination of the techniques described in this section. Putting that theory into practice, however, is often quite difficult!
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 1.2.7 Summary

    • Given any rational function with the degree of the numerator strictly less than the degree of the denominator, we can rewrite the function as a sum of terms of the form
      \begin{equation*} \frac{A}{(ax+b)^n}\ \text{or }\frac{Ax+B}{\left(ax^2 + bx +c \right)^n} \end{equation*}
      where \(A\text{,}\) \(B\text{,}\) \(a\text{,}\) \(b\text{,}\) and \(c\) are real numbers, and \(n\) is a natural number.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Which of the forms are included in the decomposition of a rational function depends entirely on the factored form of the denominator, and each of those forms can be integrated using basic integrals, substitution, and/or trigonometric substitution, possibly requiring some algebra.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Similar to how every improper fraction can be expressed as an integer plus a proper fraction, every rational function with the degree of the numerator greater than or equal to the degree of the denominator can be expressed as \(p(x)+\frac{q(x)}{r(x)}\) where \(p(x)\) is a polynomial and the degree of \(q(x)\) is strictly less than the degree of \(r(x)\text{.}\)
